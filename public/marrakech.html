The Alley That Had No Name

Written by The Weird Wanderer
Published February 14, 2023
Reading time 18 minutes
Dispatch no. No. 83
Scroll

There are cities that make you feel you know them immediately, and then there are cities that resist you for years, revealing themselves only through misreadings, wrong turns, and the particular grace of getting lost at the right time. Marrakech is emphatically the second kind of city.

I arrived on a Tuesday in February, which I mention only because Tuesdays in February have a kind of provisional quality — neither the beginning nor the middle of anything, and therefore perfect for beginning something in the wrong way. My riad was somewhere in the northern medina. I had an address that was eighteen words long and contained two street names that no longer existed and one that had never been officially named.

"Every getting lost in Marrakech is a getting lost on purpose, even when you didn't intend it. The medina conspires."

The medina of Marrakech was built before the concept of urban planning existed in any form that a modern civil engineer would recognize. It grew the way all organic things grow: following water, following light, following the logic of adjacency. One thing next to another thing because the other thing was already there. Streets narrowed because walls were built. Walls were built because land was claimed. Land was claimed because a family grew, or a business expanded, or a man decided that this was the place where he would plant his roots and let the world rearrange itself around him.

The result is a city that cannot be navigated by any form of conventional logic. The souks — the covered market streets — twist and fold into each other with what seems, to the newcomer, like malicious intent. You will try to go north and arrive facing east. You will turn left twice and end up where you started. You will turn right and find yourself in a courtyard that belongs to a private home and that has no visible exit. A man will appear and escort you out through a door you could not see.

On the second morning, I stopped trying to find my way and started trying to be found. This is a different activity, and it requires a different posture: a loosening of the grip on intention, a willingness to be where you accidentally are. I found a tea shop — or the tea shop found me, which is how it felt — just off a street that my map showed as a blank strip of yellow with no name attached.

The proprietor was a man of indeterminate age. He could have been fifty or eighty; his face had the quality of leather treated by weather and time until it had become its own kind of text. He was drinking tea from a small glass, tilting it toward the light as one might examine a jewel. He looked at me, looked at the empty chair across the low table, and said something I did not understand.

"He said something in Darija that I took to mean: you've arrived, though I'm not sure you knew you were coming. I may have been wrong about this. I held onto it anyway."

We sat and drank tea for an hour, neither of us with any particular command of the other's language, and we managed nonetheless to establish certain facts about each other: that he had lived in this neighborhood for sixty years; that his father had built the table we were sitting at; that the alley outside had no name because it had never needed one — everyone who needed to find it already knew where it was, and strangers weren't supposed to find it, and occasionally one came along and sat down and drank tea.

I was, apparently, that occasional stranger.

He refilled my glass without asking. The tea was scalding and sweet in the way that good Moroccan mint tea achieves — a sweetness that is its own kind of force, not cloying but assertive, a declaration of intention. The mint was fresh and aggressive. I burned my lips a little, which seemed correct.

Outside, the alley continued to do what alleys in the medina do: narrow, expand, fill with the sound of someone hammering somewhere distant, empty out completely for twenty minutes, then suddenly route three men carrying long planks of wood through its improbable width.

Later — I'm not sure how much later, because time in the medina operates by its own units — I tried to explain to him, in my terrible French, that I had been trying to give my experience of the city some shape, some narrative logic. That I had arrived with the assumption that the medina could be understood, mapped, mastered in the way that most Western visitors seem to assume places can be mastered: through preparation, through the accumulation of information, through the application of sufficient effort.

He listened to this carefully. He refilled my glass again.

He said, in French this time, something that I think translates roughly as: the city doesn't care what you want to understand about it. It has its own agenda.

"A city is not a problem to be solved. It is a condition to be inhabited. You inhabit it or it inhabits you, but either way you are not in charge of the terms."

I stayed another three days in Marrakech. I did not try to map the medina. I went back to the unnamed alley twice. The second time, the tea shop was there and the proprietor was there and he nodded when I appeared, and brought tea without ceremony, and we sat again in what I can only describe as a mutually comfortable silence that neither of us had earned but both of us seemed to share.

The third time, the alley wasn't there. Or — more precisely — I found an alley that looked like the alley but which contained, in the place where the tea shop should have been, a door that was firmly shut and showed no signs of ever having been a tea shop. This is, I've since come to understand, a completely ordinary thing to happen in Marrakech. The city rearranges itself while you're not looking. This is not a metaphor.

The alley at dusk — taken on the fourth morning when it had, temporarily, a different name

What I took from Marrakech — what I take, still, years later — is not knowledge about the city. It is something more like a posture. An orientation. The understanding that some places are not meant to be understood, only moved through; and that moving through them carefully, with attention to what seems incidental, is its own form of knowledge. Not the kind you can put in a guidebook, but the kind that changes how you move.
